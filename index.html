Reflections

Reflection 1: Principles of sustainability to create viable systems

Almost by definition, a truly viable system is one that remains viable across time, and that implies a degree of sustainability. Unsustainable systems don’t last the long term after all. While sustainability often refers to managing the effect on the environment, it also refers to ability to remain viable and functioning of social, economic, and cultural systems. My example below is a group project, what I am classifying as a social system.

In 2024 I took a games development elective where we had two large group tasks to complete over the semester. As I was away during the first week when groups were formed, I was placed into a group I hadn’t chosen and hadn’t met. The group was very slow to get going and several members were away on a semi-regular basis. The first assessment task had a tight deadline that required us to get a prototype early on and iterate on it each week before another scheduled class of testing. To meet this weekly deadline with such a fragmented group, I had to put massive amounts of work in to produce something worthwhile to show in class. The other group members weren’t really pulling their weight and the pace I was going at was not sustainable for me on top of the other classes I was taking that semester.

After the rushed submission for the first assessment I decided to talk with the group and tell them about how the distribution of work wasn’t very equal. To my surprise, the group had an immediate response that they knew they were under-contributing due to their own workloads and hadn’t realized how much extra work I was putting in to keep the group moving forward. After this chat, they resolved to contribute far more to the second assessment to equalize the difference in contributions. What I learned from this experience was the value in knowing your own limits of your working ability and setting clear boundaries to maintain a sustainable pace in large projects. There’s no reason to burn yourself out working on a project when you have a team that can help share the weight. If we had established these clear boundaries of contributions from the beginning, I probably wouldn’t have burned out in the first place.

Reflection 2: Professional Practice within intercultural and global contexts

I went for a Global Short exchange program to Malaysia in January 2024 and during my time there we had a final group presentation assessment before we finished. Also on this trip we were assigned buddies from the university we were attending that would show us around and offer help if needed. Over the course of the trip we became good friends with the buddies. On several occasions they recommended local experiences and locations to visit during our stay and, being from outside the culture, it was a great help to have an easily accessible source of local knowledge.

As the trip went on, my group got a little anxious about how the work on our final presentation was coming along, and so we decided to ask the buddies for guidance. They were forthcoming in sharing how assignments normally go for them and how the marking of this kind of assignment is done a lot less seriously than we were taking it. It’s common for presentations at UTS to focus on covering each of the marking criteria first with a secondary objective of not boring your audience. The opposite seemed to be true for this assigned task. After talking it through with the buddies, we decided to pivot to a more lighthearted approach to our presentation to better fit the tone that was expected of presentations at this university and this exchange program. The final presentation ended up going well and our team received plenty of cheers from our chosen approach.

While it was quite a mild incident, what I learned from this experience was the usefulness in listening to local knowledge in any matters that cross cultural boundaries. It’s easy to bring your our assumptions from your culture and think that it applies to everyone everywhere else. But if you take the time to listen and ask questions, you’ll often find that different cultures approach the same tasks differently and the easiest way to close this gap is to ask for local knowledge. In the end we parted as good friends with the buddies and still occasionally share inside jokes from our trip. My time in Malaysia taught me a lot about intercultural communication that I will take with me through my lifetime.
